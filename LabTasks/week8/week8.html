Task 2.2: Describing advantages of a time-frequency analysis over a waveform-based analysis

The biggest advantage of a time-frequency analysis over a waveform analysis is that it makes it easier to read valuable information about the specific piece of music out of it. Based on a spectrogram, which is a visual representation of the variation of frequencies over time, we can determine roughly which notes are being played in a particular song since each note corresponds to a certain frequency. A waveform analysis, on the other hand, is very information dense, but does not provide that information in an easily readable way. It gives us the amplitude, or the energy level, at a particular time point, which corresponds to loudness and the shape of the wave gives us an indication of the changes in frequency, but lacks numerical data. If we look at the spectrogram and waveform for Gradual Sunrise, for example, we can see that we cannot learn a lot about the song from the waveform, apart from the changes in dynamics over time, which might at best give us an indication of perhaps when the chorus starts and ends (assuming that the chorus is usually the loudest part of the song, at least in the rock genre). By looking at the spectrogram, on the other hand, we can get an idea of the melody of the song if we know which frequency corresponds to which pitch.
